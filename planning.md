## 1. Detection Signals

The system uses two signals, so it is not relying on only one way of judging the text.

**Signal 1 — LLM-based classification (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile)**

This signal looks at the text as a whole and asks whether it reads like something an AI model would probably generate. It is good at catching semantic patterns and broader stylistic cues that simple heuristics miss.

The Groq prompt returns a JSON object with `verdict`, `ai_probability`, and `reasoning`. We use `ai_probability` as `llm_score`, where 0 means strongly human and 1 means strongly AI.

The main limitation is that the model is still guessing from learned patterns, not ground truth. It can miss lightly edited AI text, and it can also mistake careful, formal, or non-native human writing for AI.

**Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)**

This signal focuses on structure rather than meaning. It combines three simple checks:

- Sentence length variance, since AI writing often keeps sentence lengths too even.
- Type-token ratio, since AI writing can have less vocabulary variety.
- Punctuation density and variety, since human writing is usually a little less uniform.

Each metric is normalized to a 0–1 scale, where 1 means more AI-like uniformity. The final `stylometric_score` is the average of the three.

This signal is useful, but it has a clear weakness: short texts do not give enough data for the statistics to be reliable. It also cannot detect deeper semantic AI patterns on its own.

**How the signals are combined**

The original (two-signal) plan calculated the final score as:

`combined_score = 0.6 * llm_score + 0.4 * stylometric_score`

The LLM score got more weight because it captures meaning and context, while the stylometric score acts as a secondary check. If the two disagree a lot, the final score should stay closer to the middle, because disagreement means uncertainty.

 **Update (post-implementation, before stretch features):** Once Signal 3
 (linguistic patterns) was added, the default combiner changed to an
 **equal-weight three-signal ensemble**:
 `confidence = (llm_score + stylometric_score + linguistic_score) / 3`.
 Equal voting is the more defensible combiner for three independent signals
 and gives the self-correcting "disagreement → uncertainty" behavior. The
 original `0.6 / 0.4` weighting is retained in `combine_signals()` as a
 documented two-signal fallback. See README §7 (Spec reflection) for the full
 rationale.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

The `combined_score` represents the estimated probability that the text was AI-generated.

Thresholds:

- 0.00–0.39: likely human, shown as high-confidence human
- 0.40–0.69: genuinely ambiguous, shown as uncertain
- 0.70–1.00: likely AI, shown as high-confidence AI

A score like 0.6 should not be treated as a hard verdict. It should produce hedged language, not a strong claim. The uncertain range is intentionally wider because false positives are more harmful than cautious uncertainty.

This is not statistical calibration in the strict sense. Instead, it is behavioral calibration: we test sample inputs, compare the ordering with human intuition, and adjust the weights and thresholds until the results make sense.

 **Update (validated scores):** Testing confirmed the score spreads across all
 Three bands rather than clustering. Representative results:
 - Casual human text → confidence **0.167** (`likely_human`, high confidence)
 - Short formal sentence → confidence **0.594** (`uncertain`, low confidence)
 - High-hedging AI text → confidence **0.811** (`likely_ai`, high confidence)

Full breakdowns are in README §3 (Confidence scoring).

## 3. Transparency Label Design

These are the exact labels the API should return:

- High-confidence AI: "This content shows strong indicators of AI generation. Our system is fairly confident this was created or substantially produced by an AI tool."
- Uncertain: "We can't confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. Treat this result as inconclusive."
- High-confidence human: "This content shows strong indicators of human authorship. Our system did not detect significant signs of AI generation."

The wording stays cautious on purpose. The system should never present its output like an absolute fact, and the AI label should avoid sounding accusatory. Every response should also make it clear that an appeal is possible.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

The appeal flow is designed to let the original creator challenge a classification without building a full authentication system.

- The appeal must come from the original creator, identified by `creator_id`.
- The appeal request includes `content_id` and `creator_reasoning`.

When an appeal is received:

1. The stored record for that `content_id` changes from `classified` to `under_review`.
2. A new audit log entry is added with the appeal timestamp, the creator’s explanation, and the original attribution, confidence, and signal scores.
3. The endpoint returns a confirmation JSON saying the appeal was received and is waiting for human review.

For the reviewer’s side, `GET /log` filtered to `status: "under_review"` would show the original text, both signal scores, the combined score, the label that was shown, and the creator’s reasoning. That gives enough context for a manual decision without running detection again.

Automated re-classification is out of scope.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

There are a few situations where the system may struggle:

1. Very short or repetitive human writing, such as children’s poetry, list notes, or minimalist prose, may look AI-like to the stylometric signal.
2. Lightly edited AI output can confuse both signals, which is one reason the uncertain band exists.
3. Formal academic or technical human writing may sound templated to the LLM signal, especially for non-native English speakers or careful writers.

These cases are part of the reason the system uses hedged labels and an appeal path instead of pretending it can always be certain.

## 6. Architecture Summary

The basic submission flow is straightforward. A user sends `POST /submit` with text and `creator_id`. The Flask route sends the text through the Groq-based LLM signal and the stylometric signal in parallel, combines the scores, turns the result into one of the three labels, writes the full record to the audit log, and returns the JSON response.

The appeal flow is just as direct. A user sends `POST /appeal` with a `content_id` and a reason. The app looks up the original record, switches the status to `under_review`, adds an appeal-specific audit log entry, and confirms receipt. No automatic re-classification happens.

## 8. Stretch Features (Extra Credit)

### Stretch Feature 1: Ensemble Detection (3+ Signals)

**Status: IMPLEMENTED**

Added Signal 3: Linguistic Patterns Detection measuring:
- Hedging language density (however, moreover, furthermore, etc.)
- Contraction usage (humans use more; AI avoids them)
- Formal transition phrases

**Ensemble Combination:**
```
confidence = (signal1 + signal2 + signal3) / 3
```

Each signal votes with equal weight. This makes the system more robust:
- Signal 1 catches semantic AI patterns (LLM analysis)
- Signal 2 catches structural AI patterns (uniformity, repetition)
- Signal 3 catches formulaic language patterns (hedging, formal tone)

**Test Results:**
- High-hedging AI text: Signals (0.8, 0.633, 1.0) → Combined: 0.811 
- Mixed-confidence text: Signals (0.4, 0.483, 0.5) → Combined: 0.461 (uncertain)

## 10. Known Limitations & Edge Cases

**Handled by current design:**
1. Short/repetitive text — stylometric signal struggles; LLM and linguistic signals provide backup
2. Formal human writing — LLM may flag as AI; stylometric and linguistic signals detect human patterns
3. Lightly edited AI — uncertain band (0.40-0.69) gives an honest "don't know" answer rather than false confidence

**The dominant failure mode (honest):** formal, structured *human* writing —
academic, legal, technical, and non-native-English prose — is reliably
misclassified as AI, because all three signals key off style rather than
provenance and therefore fail *in the same direction* on that kind of text. The
ensemble does not protect against this, since agreement raises confidence even
when all three are wrong for the same reason. See README §6.

**Bug found and fixed during review:** `store.save_content()` had dropped the
`linguistic_score` column from its INSERT, so Signal 3 survived in the audit log
but was stored as `NULL` on the content row, leaving the dashboard's linguistic
stat empty. Fixed by adding the column to the INSERT. See README §8.

**Future improvements:**
- Length-aware signal weighting (down-weight heuristics on short text).
- Calibrate thresholds against a labeled human/AI corpus rather than intuition.
- Analytics view of which signals disagree most (targets for improvement).
- Multi-modal support for AI-generated images or structured data.
